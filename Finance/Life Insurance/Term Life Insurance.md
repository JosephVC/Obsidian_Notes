- Term life insurance pays a death benefit if the insured dies within the specified term but offers no savings component.
	- compared generally with whole life insurance, which is more expensive but covers you as long as you live 
	- time period tends to be 10-20 years
	- when the term expires you can either renew it, convert it to permanent coverage, or just allow it to lapse
	- if you die during the term, the face value of the policy is paid out
		- that policy amount is usually not taxable
	- a policy that is not **guaranteed renewable** may be refused by the insurance company
- Policies are affordable, with premiums based on age, health, and life expectancy.
	- may require medical exams
	- usually cheapest form as it runs over a restricted time and has no permanent cash value such as permanent insurance
	- **premiums** - what you pay per month for the policy - go up based on age and other factors
		- **level premium** - fixed monthly payment for the life of the policy
- Options may include renewing the policy or converting it into permanent insurance at a higher cost.
- Term length can vary, typically ranging from 10 to 30 years; some policies offer up to 40 years.
	- **yearly renewable life insurance** - one year policies renewable each year, but costs go up as you age
- Considerations when choosing term life insurance include actuarial costs, health changes at renewal, and potential coverage conversions
- Life Insurance can be used as an investment vehicle but may not stack up well when compared to others
- **Convertible term life insurance**
	- contains a rider that allowed the current policy to be converted to a permanent plan without going through additional  underwriting or proving insurability
		- **riders** are additional benefits that can be bought and added to a basic life insurance plan
