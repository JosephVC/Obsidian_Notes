
- a ***401k withdrawal*** is taking money directly out of an account
	- these are taxed as ordinary income
- you can miss out on potential market returns
- you can take a long time to recover lost value
- both of the above assume the market doesn't wholly crash
- 
- the money withdrawn cannot be put back and you will be charged income taxes on it

- **Exceptions to the Early Withdrawal Penalties***
	- first time home buyers - up to 10k
	- disaster recovery - up to $22k
	- unreimbursed medical expenses that are greater than 7.5% of your adjusted gross income
	- being diagnosed with a terminal illness 


- ***In Service Withdrawals***
	- regular withdrawal done while still working
	- depending on plan, you might be able to remove funds through an in-service withdrawal at any time
	- you still owe taxes on this + 10% early withdrawal penalty if under 59 1/2 


- ***Hardship Withdrawal***
	- https://www.investopedia.com/retirement/relief-401k-hardship-withdrawals/
	- meant for an immediate and urgent financial need that other sources cannot meet
	- ***check with plan administrator to see if the plan even allows this***
	1. Note the need for a hardship withdrawal
	2. Document the need for the withdrawal
	3. contact the plan administrator to indicate you're ready to make the withdrawal
	4. fill the plan's required forms

	- **qualifying reasons for the withdrawal***
		- medical expenses
		- expenses related to the purchase or repair of your principal residence
		- postsecondary education (tuition, fees, room/board)
		- funeral expenses
		- preventing eviction/foreclosure 

	- IRS requirements for where the withdrawal can come from
		- direct contributions ("elective deferrals") but not the earnings on those contributions
		- contributions made by one's employer
		- matching contributions from one's employer
		- **always talk to the plan admin to see what is allowed***
